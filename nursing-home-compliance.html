Nursing Home Compliance & Patient Safety

A two-page Tableau dashboard built on 418,479 CMS nursing-home deficiency citations — from a 157 MB raw CSV, through a typed PostgreSQL schema and derived harm-level fields, to an interactive view of where care falls short and how long it takes to fix.

The question

Every U.S. nursing home is surveyed for health deficiencies, and CMS publishes the results openly. But the raw file is a flat list of hundreds of thousands of citations — hard to read as a story about where residents are most at risk and how reliably problems get corrected. I set out to turn that file into a dashboard a compliance or quality lead could actually use to spot patterns and outliers.

Data & method

Source: the CMS Provider Data Catalog, Health Deficiencies dataset (r5ix-sfxw) — a 157 MB CSV of 418,479 citations.

  • Loaded into PostgreSQL 18 with a typed schema, keeping CCN and ZIP as text so leading zeros survive; bulk-loaded via \copy.
  • Built derived fields: harm level from the CMS A–L scope-and-severity grid, CAP status (corrective-action-plan on file or not), and days-to-correct from survey to correction date.
  • Added a RANK table calculation so top-N views stay responsive to whatever filters the user applies.
  • Built a two-page Tableau dashboard with cross-sheet filter actions, click-to-filter from a US map, severity filtering, and navigation buttons between pages.

What the data shows

Corrections are usually quick — a median of 29 days from survey to correction, with the 95th percentile at 59 days. But 1,712 citations have no corrective-action plan on file, and 2.45% of those are immediate-jeopardy findings — the highest-severity band, where a resident's health or safety is at serious risk.

Judgment calls

The numbers only mean something if the data behind them is honest. Three things in this file would mislead a reader who took it at face value — so I flagged them rather than quietly smoothing them over:

The 6× "rise" in deficiencies is a coverage artifact, not a trend

Distinct facility counts grow from 40 in 2017 to 12,168 in 2024 — because the file keeps only recent survey cycles per facility, so earlier years are sparsely represented. The apparent surge is the dataset filling in, not care getting worse. I scoped the analysis to 2023–2025, where coverage is stable.

October 2025's severity "spike" is a low-volume illusion

That month has just 2,432 surveys versus a typical ~9,000, so percentage-based severity swings wildly on a small base. I flagged it rather than smoothing it, so the shape of the data stays visible.

1.5% of records correct before they're surveyed

These have a correction date earlier than the survey date — impossible in practice, so I treated them as data-quality issues and excluded them from the duration analysis rather than letting negative durations distort the medians.
